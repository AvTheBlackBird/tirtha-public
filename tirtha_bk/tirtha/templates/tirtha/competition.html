Project Tirtha - Heritage Site Imaging Competition

Project Tirtha is an academic initiative to create 3D models of heritage sites using crowdsourced images. The word Tirtha is Sanskrit for "a place of pilgrimage", and is commonly used to refer to sacred sites. Our goal is to preserve and showcase the beauty and cultural significance of heritage sites. We believe that by allowing the general public to contribute to the creation of these models, and by providing open access to these models, we can increase awareness and appreciation of these important cultural landmarks and inspire future generations to maintain them for years to come. To know more about this project, please visit the Project Tirtha website.

In this site imaging competition, the goal is to collect images of heritage sites via the Project Tirtha website, which will then be used to automatically create 3D models. This provides a chance for you to contribute to the preservation of heritage sites in your area, which are unfortunately overlooked by heritage conservation efforts, primarily due to lack of resources. Note that, metadata is maintained for all contributions to Tirtha. So, all 3D models, generated using your contributions, will credit you as a contributor. This competition additionally provides a chance to win exciting prizes for your contributions. Your contributions will be judged based on various criteria, listed under Scoring rubric below. Further details can also be found below.

Note: In case you want to propose the addition of a heritage site to the Tirtha database before contributing, please either use the form provided on the Tirtha homepage, or mail us with some details about the site (Name, location and if possible, a link to the site's Wikipedia page or another source) at project.tirtha@niser.ac.in.

Exciting prizes

Top 5 contributors on the final leaderboard will be awarded 1 custom 3D printed model each. The winners are free to submit their own models for printing (related or unrelated to Tirtha). Otherwise, the models will be of the heritage sites they contributed to. The 3D prints will be of FDM type, with max dimensions 12cm x 12cm x 12cm, in 'White' using PLA or ABS material. The winners will be contacted via email for further details.

Please note that while the competition is open to everyone, including older batches (2017, 2018), due to logistical limitations, the prizes can only be awarded to current students of NISER.

Instructions for contributing

A few guidelines are given below, but please go through the full instructions on the Tirtha website. A short video tutorial is also available here. You can propose new sites using this form or by mailing us at project.tirtha@niser.ac.in.

  1. The object of interest, i.e., a building situated at a heritage site, should be clearly visible, without obstructions, and be in focus.
  2. The images should be taken from multiple angles with 30-40% overlap.
  3. The images must be above 1080p in resolution and should contain EXIF data, mainly the camera parameters.
  4. Try to be as safe as possible while taking the images. Do not trespass or put yourself in danger.
  5. Confirm that none of the photos violate our Terms of Use or Privacy Policy

Scoring rubric

The preliminary score, as seen on the leaderboard website, is calculated as follows (\(C_i\) refers to Criteria):

\[ S_{\text{Prelim}} = 0.35 * C_1 + 0.2 * C_2 + 0.3 * C_3, \]

where,

  • \(C_1\) is the ratio of the number of images validated & used by the SfM node, to the number of images contributed by a contributor.

\[ C_1 = \frac{n({\text{Images validated}})}{n({\text{Images contributed}})} \]

  • \(C_2\) is the number of sites / latest runs contributed to, by a contributor. Note that we only consider the latest run for each site / monument for each leaderboard update.

\[ C_2 = n({\text{Sites contributed to}}) = n({\text{Latest runs contributed to}}) \]

  • For \(C_3\), we first take the weighted sum of the normalized count of extracted features & the normalized inverse median reprojection error, for each image used in the latest reconstruction run for a site. This gives us:

\[ \sigma = \sum_{i \in \text{Images used}}(0.65\times ||\text{Extracted features for }i|| + 0.35\times ||\text{1 / Median Error for }i||) \]

Then, we sum this per-run score over all the latest runs (i.e., all sites). We use min-max normalization here (per run).

\[ C_3 = \sum_{r \in \text{Latest runs}}\sigma_r \]

  • \(C_1\) is scaled and rounded up, while \(C_3\) is rounded up. Then, \(S_{\text{Prelim}}\) is calculated as above.

The final scores at the end of the competition will include another term, \(0.15 * C_4\), which will be calculated after the competition ends. \(C_4\) is a subjective score, based on the completeness of the final reconstruction & the details captured, as judged by the organizers, given as:

\[ C_4 = \sum_{s \in \text{Sites contributed to}} Q_s\text{, where }Q_s\text{ denotes a quality score, such that }Q_s \in [1, 2, 3] \]

\(C_4\) is shared by all contributors to a reconstruction run, i.e., if a run has \(C_4 = 2\), all contributors will get \(2 (\times 0.15)\) for this criterion. It follows a 3-point scale, with 1 being the lowest & 3 being the highest, as detailed below:

  • 1: Reconstruction is mostly incomplete, with many large holes & missing details.
  • 2: Reconstruction is mostly complete, with some holes & missing details, or, reconstruction is mostly incomplete, but with a lot of details captured.
  • 3: Reconstruction is mostly complete, with almost no holes & most details captured.

The overall score of a contributor will be calculated as follows:

\[ S_{\text{Final}} = S_{\text{Prelim}} + 0.15 * C_4 \]
